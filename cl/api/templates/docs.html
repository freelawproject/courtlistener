CourtListener APIs and Bulk Legal Data

We currently have four services available to researchers and organizations using our platform:

  1. For those that need the fastest and most granular form of our data, we offer our database replication service. This service provides a logical replica of our PostgreSQL database onto a server where you can query it.

  2. If you are interested in obtaining large quantities of our data, we recommend using bulk data files. These provide compressed JSON files of our opinion and people databases, by object type and jurisdiction

  3. For usages that require less data, such as keeping up to date with the latest filings, accessing a subset of the data or performing RECAP uploads, we recommend using our REST APIs.

  4. Finally, we also offer custom data services and custom bulk file creation, with hourly and project-based rates.

We make these services available to the public according to means-based pricing. This allows a variety of researchers, journalists, and organizations to access this information.

The goal of these charges is to provide ongoing support for these APIs and for Free Law Project, the 501(c)(3) non-profit supporting CourtListener and RECAP. We hope you'll agree that that is an important cause.

Depending on what makes sense for the organization and the project, support for Free Law Project can take the form of monthly subscriptions, one time fees for service, or revenue share agreements.

We're Proud of our Data

Over the past several years, we have made a significant investment in making our data the best on the open Web. Among the minor enhancements we make on a regular basis, we've made significant improvements to our data that you won't find in other sources:

  1. We've added exact dates from the library of congress to our collection of older Supreme Court cases, enhancing thousands of cases beyond what's available anywhere else.
  2. When we imported the Resource.org data, we manually corrected more than ten thousand omissions or errors in the data, ensuring that it's complete and accurate.
  3. Our data has the citation network, showing which cases reference each other, saving you the effort of building and testing a citation finding and matching algorithm yourself.
  4. We complete complicated cleanup routines on the data, eliminating and fixing errant abbreviations like 'et al', 'U.S.A.' and the like.
  5. The data on CourtListener is a combination of many partial sources such as court websites, Public.Resource.Org, and a donation from Lawbox LLC. This means that we have the best parts of all of our sources.
  6. We help identify opinions that should be private. Many people don't want their case on the Web and have contacted us. In our data we provide a field indicating which cases should be kept private. In addition, we provide research about how to keep these cases out of search engines (more detail at right).

You can learn more about the data that we have on our coverage page.

Privacy of Court Materials

As is explained in our removal policy, we block public search engines from indexing many of the cases available in our data. We do this through a combination of automatic and manual procedures.

As you work with this data, it is essential that you be cognizant of the privacy and safety issues involved with legal documents. This impacts real people with real privacy concerns. This includes jailed confidential informants, the victims of stalkers, foreign citizens with rejected asylum requests, children who were named incidentally in a case about their parent's wrongdoings, exhonorated individuals, and the list goes on.

If you intend to use this data on the Internet, carefully consider how you will protect these people and respect the privacy wishes of the parties involved. Please read our privacy policy for our approach to this problem and identify the "blocked" field in each case. This field describes whether we have blocked search engines from indexing the case. The simple thing for you to do is to also block such cases.

Please also note that robots.txt is a wholly insufficient way to block court documents from search engines. For a better technical approach, please see the blog entries below.

  • Support for x-robots-tag and robots HTML meta tag
  • Further privacy protections at CourtListener
